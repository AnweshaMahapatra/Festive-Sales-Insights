# Business Questions & Insights
## Stakeholder Review — AtliQ Mart Promotional Performance Analysis

*10 strategic questions grounded in the actual data from the Diwali 2023 and Sankranti 2024 campaigns.*

---

### Q1. Which state witnessed the highest increase in revenue — and what explains its performance?

**Answer: Madurai at 120% IR% — outperforming cities with far larger store networks.**

Madurai leads all 10 cities in the network on IR%, despite having only 4 stores compared to Bengaluru's 10. The 120% figure means Madurai generated 20% more revenue during the promotion period than its baseline — a clean, meaningful lift.

What makes this analytically interesting is that Chennai (116.84%) and Bengaluru (116.05%) — both larger markets with more stores — trail Madurai. This suggests the performance gap is not about scale or brand presence. It is about promotional fit: the right promotion types reaching the right customer base in the right market. Understanding Madurai's specific promotion mix and store execution and testing whether it can be replicated in Mangalore (98.82%) or Visakhapatnam (94.39%) is a direct action item.

---

### Q2. Which promotion resulted in the highest incremental revenue?

**Answer: 500 Cashback — ₹91M IR, driven by Diwali's ₹77M contribution.**

500 Cashback is the revenue champion. At ₹91M IR across both campaigns, it outperformed BOGOF (₹69M) despite generating only 41K incremental units versus BOGOF's 372K. The revenue-per-incremental-unit ratio tells the story clearly: 500 Cashback generates roughly ₹2,220 per incremental unit moved; BOGOF generates roughly ₹185.

The mechanics explain why. 500 Cashback on a ₹1,200 product means the customer pays ₹700 after cashback — a meaningful saving, but the business still retains ₹700 in revenue. BOGOF on the same product means the business receives ₹600 (half price) while delivering two units. 500 Cashback protects revenue while creating a compelling customer incentive. For premium categories like Home Appliances, where customers have high willingness to spend but respond to perceived value, 500 Cashback is structurally the superior promotion type.

---

### Q3. Which promotion resulted in the lowest incremental revenue — and should it be retired?

**Answer: 25% OFF at –₹3M IR — and yes, all three discount promos should be reconsidered.**

25% OFF was the worst-performing promotion on IR (–₹3M), followed by 33% OFF (–₹2M) and 50% OFF (–₹1M). All three are revenue-negative. This is not a marginal underperformance — these promotions actively destroyed value across both campaigns.

The root cause is math: a 33% discount requires 50% more volume just to break even on revenue. A 50% discount requires 100% more volume. Neither threshold was reached. The volume attracted by these discount levels was real but insufficient to offset the pricing concession given.

Before retiring them entirely, the right question is whether the issue is the discount depth, the product selection, or the campaign context. A 25% OFF promotion on a high-elasticity product in a price-sensitive market might work. Applying it broadly across the portfolio during a premium festive period where customers are already motivated to spend — as appears to have happened here — does not.

---

### Q4. Which product showed the highest lift in revenue — and what does it tell us about the portfolio?

**Answer: Atliq Waterproof Immersion Rod at 266.19% IR% — and it confirms Home Appliances as the promotional anchor category.**

The Waterproof Immersion Rod, priced at ₹1,190 and featured in BOGOF, generated the highest IR% of any product across both campaigns. The Atliq High Glo 15W LED Bulb followed at 262.98%. Both are Home Appliances.

The top 4 products by IR% are all either Home Appliances or Home Care. This is not a coincidence — it is a category pattern. Products in these categories sit at a price point where customers notice promotions, respond to them, and buy in higher quantities when incentivised. The promotional elasticity is high.

This has direct implications for budget allocation in the next campaign: promotional investment in Home Appliances generates the highest return per rupee spent. Diversifying promotional spend across categories with low elasticity (Grocery & Staples at 18.05% ISU% during Diwali) at the expense of Home Appliances would be a strategic misallocation.

---

### Q5. Which product showed the lowest lift — and what does that mean for how we promote it?

**Answer: Personal Care category at –34.20% IR% — and it should be removed from discount-based promotions entirely.**

Across the category analysis, Personal Care was the only revenue-negative category at –34.20% IR%. Its ISU% was a modest 24.50% — meaning a small volume increase accompanied a significant revenue decline. Customers bought slightly more, but only because prices were cut enough to destroy the revenue gain from that volume.

Personal Care customers in AtliQ Mart's network appear to be habitual buyers — they purchase regardless of promotion, so discounts primarily cannibalise full-price purchases rather than expand the customer base. The right response is not to promote Personal Care harder; it is to stop discounting it and either deprioritise it in the promotional calendar or shift to non-discount mechanics like loyalty points or bundle inclusions.

---

### Q6. Between Diwali and Sankranti, which campaign should receive greater investment in future?

**Answer: Diwali — ₹89M IR vs ₹66M IR — because its promotion mix is more revenue-efficient.**

Diwali generated more incremental revenue (₹89M vs ₹66M) and did so primarily through 500 Cashback (₹77M IR from 33.98K ISU) — the highest revenue-per-unit promotion type. Sankranti's ₹66M IR came largely from BOGOF (313.43K ISU, ₹53M IR) — high volume, lower revenue efficiency.

The implication is not that Sankranti should be deprioritised entirely, but that its promotional mix should be refined. If Sankranti's campaign applied more 500 Cashback mechanics (particularly on Home Appliances) and fewer flat percentage discounts, its revenue outcome would improve significantly. Diwali's mix is already closer to optimal; Sankranti's has more room to improve.

---

### Q7. BOGOF generated 372K incremental units — more than all other promotion types combined. Is that success?

**Answer: It depends entirely on the objective — and that objective needs to be defined before the next campaign.**

372K ISU is a remarkable volume number. For inventory management — clearing overstock, driving footfall, generating trial of new products — BOGOF delivered results no other promotion type came close to matching. Sankranti BOGOF alone generated **313K ISU**, moving product through the network at scale.

But BOGOF's IR was ₹69M — lower than 500 Cashback's ₹91M on 41K ISU. If the campaign objective is revenue, BOGOF is not the lead promotion. If the objective is volume, clearance, or footfall, BOGOF is the best tool available.

The problem is that campaigns often don't define their objective precisely enough before launch. "Run a promotion" is not an objective. "Maximise incremental revenue from Home Appliances during Diwali" leads to 500 Cashback on high-ticket items. "Clear Grocery & Staples inventory before Sankranti" leads to BOGOF on staples. The data is now available to make those decisions correctly. Whether the planning process uses it is a different question.

---

### Q8. Bengaluru has 10 stores — the most in the network — but its IR% is only 116.05%. Is that a problem?

**Answer: It's underperformance relative to smaller markets, but it reflects a structural reality worth understanding.**

Bengaluru's 116.05% IR% places it third behind Madurai (120%) and Chennai (116.84%), despite having 25% more stores than Chennai and 2.5× more than Madurai. In absolute revenue terms, Bengaluru almost certainly generates more incremental revenue than Madurai — the percentage just reflects proportional lift over baseline.

The more relevant question is: given Bengaluru's 10-store footprint and the promotional investment allocated to it, is 116% the right return? And are specific Bengaluru stores dragging down the city average? The Bottom 10 stores list includes several STBLR-coded entries — which means some Bengaluru stores are underperforming their city peers significantly. A store-level audit of those specific STBLR bottom performers, rather than a city-level response, is the right diagnostic action.

---

### Q9. Home Appliances showed 628.78% ISU% — more than any other category. How should that change our store inventory planning?

**Answer: It should trigger a pre-promotion inventory buffer for Home Appliances in every city before the next festive campaign.**

A 628.78% ISU% during the post-promo period means Home Appliances stores moved more than 7× their baseline unit volume. This level of demand surge creates two operational risks: stockouts (losing sales because inventory ran out mid-campaign) and overstock (if the promotion underperforms and excess inventory was ordered speculatively).

Given that this is now a confirmed, repeatable pattern — Home Appliances responded at 265.21% IR% and 628.78% ISU% — it is no longer speculative. The next Diwali or Sankranti campaign should trigger a deliberate pre-promotion inventory build for Home Appliances, particularly for the top IR% products (Immersion Rod, LED Bulb). The cost of holding additional inventory is far lower than the cost of stockouts during a 7× demand event.

---

### Q10. If only one change could be made to improve the next campaign's performance, what should it be?

**Answer: Remove all three discount-based promotion types (25% OFF, 33% OFF, 50% OFF) and reallocate that budget to 500 Cashback on Home Appliances and Home Care SKUs.**

The three discount promotions destroyed ₹6M in revenue combined. That is money the business spent — in the form of margin concessions — to generate a net negative outcome. Removing them from the next campaign immediately eliminates ₹6M in value destruction.

Reallocating the associated promotional budget to 500 Cashback on Home Appliances and Home Care is not a speculative decision. The data from both Diwali and Sankranti confirms that 500 Cashback on these categories generates the highest IR% in the portfolio. The Waterproof Immersion Rod (266.19% IR%), LED Bulb (262.98%), Double Bedsheet Set (258.27%), and Curtains (255.34%) are all waiting for exactly this kind of well-funded promotion.

One structural change. The direction is clear. The data is already there to support it.
